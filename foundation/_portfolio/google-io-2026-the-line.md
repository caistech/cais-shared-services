## The one idea: "the line"

I/O 2026 wasn't 100 launches — it was **one strategic move announced 100 ways**: Google drew a
horizontal line through the AI stack and made **everything below it free, built-in, gone as a
business.** The presenter's whole thesis, and it's ours too:

| BELOW the line — now commodity / free | ABOVE the line — structurally can't be commoditised |
|---|---|
| Raw access to a smart model | A workflow built **deep into one specific industry** |
| A generic agent that browses + runs code (ships in 1 line) | **Proprietary context** — your data, your customers, your judgment |
| Generic image / video / music generation | **Trust where being wrong is expensive** (healthcare, legal, finance) |
| An app built for a single prompt | **Distribution you actually own** |
| Thin wrappers | **A point of view a model can't average its way into** |

**The test (memorise this — it's the cleanest one-liner we have):**
> *"If Google shipped a free, decent version of what I sell tomorrow, would I still have customers?"*
> Yes → above the line. No → that's your to-do list, not a disaster.

**The reframe that matters most:** everything below the line is **not a threat — it's free fuel.**
"Don't fight Google below the line; strip-mine the free layer to build something above it. Use the
commodity, build the unique angle — *and the order matters.*" This is exactly our "Google
commoditised capabilities, not our assembly" point.

## What we should actually learn from it (business types & models)

**1. The "line test" is a sharper gate than our rubric currently phrases.** Adopt it verbatim as the
plain-English framing of `BUSINESS_MODEL.md` §5 D5 (build-fit) + the moat. Every product card could
carry a one-line answer to *"if Gemini shipped this free tomorrow, who still pays us and why?"* —
that answer **is** the above-the-line thesis. Worth adding as a prompt on the methodology card.

**2. Agent-readability (his System 6) is the one "almost no one is acting on" — we now are.** He
calls it possibly the most important system: *"soon the thing visiting your website won't be a
person — it'll be that person's AI agent… find 3–4 actions that make you money (book a call, get a
quote, start a trial) and make sure an agent can complete them start-to-finish. For 15 years we
optimised for Google's crawler; this is that again, for agents. Founders who do it early get found;
the ones who wait go invisible and won't know why."* → This validates rubric **#42** *and* pushes us
to take the **operability** layer (WebMCP tools on the money-making actions) seriously, not just
discoverability. It's the §11 Layer-2 case, in his words.

**3. The two monetisation paths — note the alignment AND the divergence from our model.**
- **Path 1 — your own business:** install the systems where you bleed the most time. (= our internal
  factory leverage; the 12 systems are our `@caistech` substrate + agent layer.)
- **Path 2 — "the one most will sleep on": every system is a service you can sell.** *"Millions of
  small businesses will never watch an I/O recap and don't know AI Studio exists. They'll happily pay
  someone to set up the market monitor, build the dashboard, wire the AI chief of staff. You're not
  selling AI — you're selling the result: their week back, pipeline visible, admin gone. That's the
  model behind my agency."*
  - **Alignment:** strong support for lanes 2–3 (studio-in-residence, contract builds) and for
    selling the *result*, not the AI.
  - **Divergence (important):** his Path 2 is a **direct-to-SMB agency**. **Ours is explicitly
    distributor-first (Rule 15): we sell to operators who already have a book of SMBs and clip per
    end-user — we do NOT acquire/serve SMBs one-by-one.** His model is the thing our model is designed
    to *beat on leverage* (one agency serving SMBs linearly vs. one substrate × many distributors ×
    their books). So: take his "sell the result / install-as-a-service" framing, **route it through a
    distributor**, don't copy the 1:1 agency shape. The agency path doesn't compound; the distributor
    path does.

**4. His "above the line" list is our moat list, almost verbatim** — vertical depth, proprietary
context, trust-in-regulated, owned distribution, non-average POV. Our REGULATED-tier products
(mmcbuild, ndis-sda, r&d-tax, f2k-checkpoint) and the methodology/distributor pipeline are precisely
these. Reassuring corroboration that the portfolio is structurally on the right side — *provided* we
keep treating the commodity layer as fuel and don't let any product's value rest on a below-the-line
capability alone.

**5. On-device / regulated as the sharpest above-the-line play (his System 12, LiteRT-LM).** "Build
for the places the cloud isn't welcome — healthcare, legal, finance — data never leaves the device."
A real structural angle for the compliance engine (Engine 4) and a differentiator the free layer
*sharpens* rather than erases. Worth a feasibility note for the regulated lane.

**Net:** the video is a sales funnel wrapped around a correct strategic spine. The spine is already
our `BUSINESS_MODEL.md`. The three things worth importing: (a) the **"would I still have customers"
line test** as a card prompt, (b) **agent operability** (not just discoverability) as real GTM, and
(c) the **"sell the result, through a distributor"** sharpening of lanes 2–3.

There's a specific kind of AI founder who lost their business this week. They just don't know it yet. And it's not the ones who ignored AI. I believe it's the ones who built their whole company on top of it. Because last week at Google IO 2026, Google did not launch a product or a few. What they did was that they drew a line. A line straight through the middle of the AI industry, and everything on one side of the line — generic AI agents, thin wrappers, simple app builders, basic content tools — Google basically just made them free, built in, gone as a business. If that's what you were selling, unfortunately, that's the bad news. But here's the good news, and it's bigger. Everything that Google just made free is now fuel. So I'm going to share with you 12 systems that you can run today that used to need a team and a budget and months of work, but now that's not the case.

IO 2026 was not 100 product launches. It was one strategic move announced 100 ways. For 2 years, the AI business looked like a gold rush. Anyone could grab a model, wrap a nice interface around it, and sell it. Execution was scarce, so wrappers had value. But Google basically just ended that. They put their fast new model, Gemini 3.5 Flash, inside Search, inside Chrome, inside Workspace, inside Android. They turned search into something that builds you a dashboard instead of 10 blue links. They made it so a single line of code spins up a full AI agent — sandbox, browser, memory. They shipped agents that monitor the web for you, agents that run your inbox, agents that edit your videos by conversation. Every one of those was a startup or more — some funded — but Google just turned them into features, free. Google says its AI mode in search has now over a billion users a month. That is the single biggest distribution pipe on the internet, and Google just pointed it at every workflow that you might have been planning to charge for. This was the floor of the AI industry basically dropping. And whether that's a threat or the best news you'll hear all year depends on exactly one thing: where your business is standing right now.

Below the line is everything that is now a commodity. Raw access to a smart model is a commodity. A generic agent that can browse and run code — it ships in one line of code. Generic image, video, music generation — commodity. An app built for a single prompt — commodity. If your business is one of those things, you're now competing with a free feature that has a billion-user front door. Above the line is everything Google did not and structurally cannot commoditize: a workflow built deep into one specific industry; proprietary context — your data, your customers, your judgment; trust in the markets where being wrong is expensive like healthcare, legal, finance; distribution that you actually own; a point of view that a model cannot generate because models are trained on average and the average is exactly what you're refusing to be. Stop selling what is below the line and start using it. Everything below the line just became free leverage, free fuel. You want to be the one strip-mining the free layer to build something above it. Use the commodity, build the unique competitive angle, and the order matters.

[Build faster] System 1 — prompt to product. You describe the thing you want to build in plain English. Google AI Studio turns that into a working prototype. Gemini 3.5 Flash handles the reasoning and the code, and Antigravity lets you keep building and shipping it. A prototype is not a company — this collapses the cost of testing an idea, not the cost of being right about which idea. System 2 — your AI product team. Antigravity 2.0 lets you run several AI agents at once in parallel: one builds the app, one writes the landing page, one designs brand assets, one hunts for bugs. Your bottleneck was never just the building — it was that you can only do one thing at a time. System 3 — software from your spreadsheets. AI Studio can read the mess — spreadsheets, scattered docs, thousands of emails — and turn it into actual internal tools: a real dashboard from your sales sheet, a simple client portal, an ops tool. The value isn't new information — it's information your team can finally see and act on.

[Sell smarter] System 4 — the analyst that never sleeps. Google is rolling out Search Information Agents: agents that run in the background, watch the web, and report back only when something actually changes. Set a standing instruction — "watch my three competitors, tell me when one changes pricing or launches something" — and forget about it until it pings you. Every founder does this research; almost none do it continuously, because continuously is boring — but now boring is free. System 5 — a decision tool, not a document. Search can now build you an interface on the spot: a live dashboard, a calculator, a comparison table generated from your question. You don't get a paragraph back, you get a little model you can poke at. This turns search from a place you read into a place that lets you think. System 6 — make your business agent-readable. Soon the thing visiting your website will not be a person — it'll be that person's AI agent, sent to book, to buy, to compare, or to get a quote on their behalf. For 15 years we optimized websites for Google's crawler; this is that again, but for agents. Daily Brief gives you a morning digest — urgent emails, today's meetings, dropped follow-ups, what's at risk.
